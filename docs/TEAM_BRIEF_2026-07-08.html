Resonance Tree -- Lighting Workstream

About 150 lanterns that light themselves

Where the light system stands as of July 8: what we've built and proven on the bench (with dates and measurements), how the fixtures work and talk to each other, and the plan that gets everything into the container on August 21.

01 · What & why

The fleet, in one picture

The Resonance Tree gets a fleet of small, identical, fully wireless light fixtures. Each one is its own complete system: a solar panel or battery, a charge controller, a tiny computer with a radio, an LED, and (on most) sensors. No power lines, no data lines, no pairing. Any fixture can swap with any other in about 30 seconds, and where each one hangs is a free choice we can make on-site, because nothing is wired to anything.

The current plan is 150–152 fixtures in four classes (counts are tentative until installation -- that flexibility is a feature, not indecision):

Everything is built to be reused: the same fixtures light the 2026 tree and the 2027 conch-shell expansion, and everything they measure this year (sun, dust, battery behavior, failures) becomes design data for next year.

02 · Anatomy

What one fixture is

The archetype is the hanging downlight: a bamboo lantern body (made by Bamboo Pure in Bali) wearing a sealed "solar hat" that holds the whole machine, dust-tight. The hat bodies are commercial enclosures bought at fleet scale (172 of them, in two sizes); Steve owns turning boxes into hats -- panel mounts, the bamboo clamp, the gasketed USB-C port, sensor windows, and gobo positioning. The 16 chandelier lights skip the hat entirely: a carpenter on the team builds their shared housing.

Gobo patterns: the community-submission program was pulled for time. The plan now is a set of in-house designs plus generative patterns derived from the leaves of the actual bamboo species used in the tree -- so the light the tree casts is, literally, its own foliage.

04 · Network

How 150 fixtures talk with no infrastructure

The fixtures use ESP-NOW -- a radio protocol where every fixture simply broadcasts small "here's my state" packets a couple of times per second, and everyone nearby hears them. There is no hub, no WiFi network, no pairing, nothing to configure at the tree. If a fixture dies, nothing else notices or cares. That's what makes the 30-second swap (and free placement) possible.

Range and robustness were tested physically: the link held from a house, across a yard, and through an oak tree; the bamboo body is essentially transparent to radio (the solar panel is the only real blocker, which the hat design accounts for). The wireless-first design is also creative headroom -- neighbor-aware ripple effects, a carried "wand" the tree reacts to, synchronized choir moments, the bridge-directed sound-reactive mode above -- all possible later without touching hardware. (The bridge already exists on the bench: it's how test data gets logged today.)

05 · Power

A fixture's day, and the safety net under it

Each fixture lives a fully autonomous daily loop. The interesting engineering is at the bottom of the battery: the cutoff voltages aren't folklore, they come from a measured discharge map of the exact production cell under the heaviest realistic load.

  • The battery is honest and mapped. The production cell (a large LiFePO4 chosen for heat tolerance and a two-year life) delivers ~5.75 Ah measured; ~5.1 Ah of that is usable above the safety floor. First instability appears at 2.69 V -- with 99% of usable energy already delivered.
  • Overnight survival is cheap. Sleeping through the night with hourly check-ins costs ~50 mAh -- about 1% of the cell.
  • Solar has one trap, and it's guarded. Connecting a panel in bright sun can latch the charger off until the input is physically removed. A small firmware guard now detects and clears that state automatically; it ships in every charging build.
  • LED wiring: settled by measurement. Decided Both LED types run off the board's switchable 3.3 V rail -- which doubles as a rock-solid hard kill switch. A battery-direct feed looked +33% brighter in early fat-wire tests, but a July 11 instrumented A/B through production-realistic cabling inverted that: the rail wins on every color (+2.5% mean). Simpler AND brighter -- one harness, one pinout. (The boost converter was measured thoroughly and shelved too.)
  • Still open: the per-role nightly energy budget, and the uplight/chandelier power choice -- dedicated panel vs a big 20 Ah cell that lasts the whole week without sun. That cell passed its first verification July 12 (19,412 mAh, 97% of label); a second sample confirms before the bulk buy. Open

06 · Maintenance

Fixing fixtures without touching them

The rule we designed to: a lantern never comes off the tree for software. Three layers make that real, and all three are validated on hardware:

  • Wireless updates, in parallel. A radio ping sends any fixture (or all of them) into maintenance mode on a portable router; a laptop then updates many at once. Measured: 3 boards in 18 seconds parallel vs 44 sequential -- the fleet updates in minutes, not hours.
  • Bad updates un-install themselves. Every new firmware must pass a self-test on boot; if it fails -- or crashes -- the fixture automatically reverts to the last good version. Proven with a deliberately broken image.
  • It works even on a nearly dead battery. Updates succeeded down to ~3.1 V on battery alone, 2.9 V with sun on the panel, and 2.5 V on USB. And if everything else fails, USB is always the boring recovery path -- every fixture gets a waterproof panel-mount USB-C port on its hat (150 bought July 10), so rescue and charging never require opening the enclosure.

07 · Interactivity

Sensing people and wind (and maybe making sound)

Most fixtures carry a tiny accelerometer (sway and motion energy -- the tree's own movement becomes an input) and a small depth sensor: downward on the hanging downlights to notice someone standing beneath, outward on the perimeter lights to catch passers-by. Chosen specifically because they need zero per-unit calibration -- a hard requirement at 150 units. With bench units included we hold 150 of each.

This is art, not security: ~80% detection is a complete success. The product is the choreography -- a lantern noticing you and the reaction rippling outward through the mesh to its neighbors. A working demo already exists: a swaying fixture drives its LED color from the fused motion + depth signal (bench-validated July 6, including the subtle geometry of a lantern that spins while it swings).

Noisemakers: hardware secured, verdict still open. Open Candidates benched: relay clickers (a lovely mechanical tick), simple beeps, buzzers, a small speaker running a custom "organic percussion" synth (bamboo knocks, chimes, water drips -- proven clean on hardware), and the one Ben leans toward: a tiny solenoid mallet that knocks the bamboo itself, making the lantern the instrument. The mallet's first bench run (July 10) fired 815 strikes without a single hiccup, and fleet-scale parts are already in transit -- 150 solenoids (two voltage variants to compare) and 110 driver boards -- so whichever sound wins, the hardware can ship. Everything stays on the table, including the humble beep, until more ears weigh in. Imagine 150 of anything rippling a sound through the tree.
