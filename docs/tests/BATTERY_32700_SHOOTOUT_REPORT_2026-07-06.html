How P lost

The label was never plausible. At 136 g, P weighs the same as a genuine 6 Ah cell — a genuine 7.2 Ah in this format would need ~20% more energy per gram than a cell that already measures at 95% of its rating, which is not a thing LFP does. The busted label was corroborated three independent ways before the discharge even finished: weight parity, 2.3× internal resistance on the charger’s IR readout, and Off-Grid Garage’s cycler test of the same cell (5,450 mAh — within 3.4% of our 5,643 despite a completely different load profile).

The subtler finding is where it loses. Down to a 2.5 V lab cutoff, P delivers a near-tie (−1.9%). But capacity below ~2.9 V is fictional for this product — the lantern firmware floors at 2.9–3.18 V to protect the cell and the rail. P’s high internal resistance pulls its voltage-under-load through the 3.0 V floor at 4.83 h against F’s 6.19 h, surrendering 15.5% of product-usable capacity. High IR converts label mAh into heat and an early knee.

Verdict

Buy the fullbattery 6 Ah. Skip the Palowextra. P delivers 15.5% less usable capacity at ~$1 more per cell — $1.40 per usable Ah against F’s $0.99. There is no price at parity that rescues a worse cell with a fake label from an unaccountable supplier.

The planned cycle-2 rig swap is deliberately skipped: the rig confound is worth at most ~8% on load current, the observed usable-capacity gap is 15.5% in the other cell’s favor, and three independent corroborations point the same direction. Remaining caveats, stated honestly: n=1 per cell, single temperature (79.9 °F), and P ran on the slightly hungrier rig. None of them is anywhere near large enough to flip the decision.

What we got for free

  • The production cell is now qualified at n=2. This F delivered 5,752 mAh against June 11’s 5,726 from a different cell, board, and day — a +0.5% reproduction. The 75-unit purchase stands validated at $0.89/delivered-Ah.
  • The fuel-gauge current bias is a chip trait, not a board quirk. The MAX17260 read +9.3% and +8.1% high on two different boards tonight — the 7th and 8th consecutive replications of the +8±1% bias. Treat /1.08 as a universal correction.
  • Both cells end in a brownout rattle below ~2.6 V (31 and 35 resets) — a state-difference artifact we initially misread as a P-only failure mode. It lives far below the product floor and is informational only.
  • Gauge SOC swept 98→0% on both cells while the plateau kept it useless in between — one more confirmation that LFP state-of-charge must come from coulomb counting, not voltage.

Method, briefly

Both boards ran identical firmware (power_bench, NeoHEX 37px white val224, charging disabled, battery floor lowered to 2.3 V) and were charged only on a Nitecore UMS4 LFP profile — no USB touched either board mid-run, the confound that invalidated a previous soak integral. Ground truth is a 4-channel INA219 monitor (10 mΩ shunts) on a standalone KB2040, one channel high-side in each battery lead and each LED line; a serial-tee (ina_logger.py) let both afk_discharge.py instances share the monitor. Runs sampled every 10 s, integrating INA current (truth) and gauge current (cross-check) to a 2.5 V under-load cutoff, two consecutive samples required.

Before the runs, a green-pulse diagnostic (ina_mapcheck.py) verified every INA channel’s rig assignment and shunt polarity against two references — it caught three reversed shunts and one crossed rig-label in the rat’s nest before they could touch the data. Re-integration by afk_analyze.py matches the live integrals within ~2 mAh; corrupt-but-parseable INA samples (the June failure mode) were filtered at ingest. One known artifact: INA bus_v reads ~0.16 V low with battery-only rigs (floating ground reference through the QT chain); all voltage decisions used the gauge’s reading.
